03
Color System
Two colors. No exceptions. This constraint is what makes the work feel unified and confident.
Navy
#1a2f42
Primary. Headlines, backgrounds, text on light. This is the brand color—use it confidently.
Bone
#f5f2eb
Secondary. Backgrounds, contrast panels. Warmer than white—feels more premium and approachable.
  • ✓ Use opacity for hierarchy. Navy at 100%, 50%, 25% creates levels without adding colors.
  • ✓ High contrast only. Navy on Bone, White on Navy. Never Navy on Navy or Bone on Bone.
  • ✗ Never add accent colors. No red, no blue, no "just a little green." Two colors.
  • ✗ Never use pure black or pure white. Navy is our dark. Bone is our light.
04
Typography
Three typefaces with clear roles. Never mix their jobs.
DM Sans — Headlines & Numbers
$547M in results.
Weight: 900 (Black) only for headlines. Use for: All headlines, statistics, CTAs, anything that needs to hit hard. Tracking: -0.03em to -0.04em (tighten it up).
Instrument Serif — Philosophy & Quotes
Ambition needs momentum.
Weight: Regular Italic only. Use for: Philosophical statements, client quotes, subheads that need warmth. Never: Use for headlines or CTAs.
JetBrains Mono — Labels & Meta
Your Business People — norcalsbdc.org
Weight: 600-700. Use for: Section labels, categories, URLs, small technical text. Always: Uppercase, letterspaced (+0.1em to +0.15em).

  • ✓ Split = Contrast. Use for before/after, cost/value, problem/solution. Always Bone left, Navy right (or reverse).

07
Spacing & Tension
Whitespace is not empty—it's active. Use it to create tension and focus.
  • ✓ Let type breathe. Headlines need room. Don't crowd them with other elements.
  • ✓ Push to edges. Type can start at the margin or even bleed slightly. This creates energy.
  • ✓ Anchor corners. Content should pull toward opposite corners—creates diagonal tension.
  • ✗ Never center everything. If the headline is centered, the CTA should be left or right. Create asymmetry.
  • ✗ Never fill the space. If you feel like something is missing, it's probably right. Resist the urge to add.

One of our signature moves: dim the setup, punch the payoff.

The first line sets up. The second line delivers. By dimming the setup (opacity: 25-40%), the payoff hits harder. Use this for:
  • ✓ Stacked headlines where the second line is the point
  • ✓ Before/after journeys where "after" is the hero
  • ✓ Labels and categories that shouldn't compete with content
09
Critical Do's and Don'ts
These rules are non-negotiable. If you're unsure, refer back to this list.
✓ Do
  • ✓ Make the headline the biggest thing by far
  • ✓ Use serif italic for warmth, sans bold for impact
  • ✓ Leave uncomfortable amounts of whitespace
  • ✓ Put the URL and tagline in mono, small, dimmed
  • ✓ Use the arrow (→) after CTAs
  • ✓ Tighten headline tracking (-0.03em)
  • ✓ Let type touch or bleed off edges
✗ Don't
  • ✗ Add photography or illustrations
  • ✗ Use more than two colors
  • ✗ Make everything the same size
  • ✗ Use drop shadows or gradients
  • ✗ Add decorative elements or icons
  • ✗ Use more than 3-4 text elements per piece
  • ✗ Center-align body copy
10
Applying to Other Formats
The system scales. Here's how to think about different applications.
Social Posts
One message per post.
Don't try to fit the whole stack. Pick Hook OR Proof OR Philosophy. Let each post do one job well.
Flyers / One-Pagers
Split the page.
Use the split layout vertically. Top half = hook. Bottom half = proof + CTA. Or left/right for horizontal formats.
Event Banners
Scale up the type.
Whatever size feels too big—go bigger. From 20 feet away, only the headline matters. Everything else is texture.
Email Headers
Navy background, white type.
Keep it simple. One line. "Add momentum." or "$0 Advice. Real results." That's it.
When In Doubt
Ask yourself: "Would Paula Scher think this is bold enough?" If no, make it bigger. If still no, take something away. The power is in the contrast—big vs. small, dark vs. light, bold vs. dim. Not in adding more.
11
Questions?
This system is intentionally constrained. The constraints are the point—they create consistency and impact. If something doesn't fit these rules, ask whether you're solving the right problem, or whether the system needs to flex.
